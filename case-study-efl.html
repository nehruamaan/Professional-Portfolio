AI Startup Evaluation Framework

Designed a 5-dimension structured framework to assess the scalability, viability, and investment-readiness of early-stage AI startups at Endless Frontier Labs.

Role: Venture Analyst
Duration: May – Sept 2025
Coverage: 5 Deep-Tech Startups
Focus: AI / Deep Tech
Contents
Context Problem Approach The Framework Application Impact Reflection

Context

Endless Frontier Labs (EFL) is a New York-based deep-tech accelerator and venture program supporting early-stage startups at the frontier of AI, biotech, and advanced computing. In the summer of 2025, the program was actively evaluating a new cohort of AI-native startups across diverse verticals — from infrastructure to enterprise software to applied research.

The challenge wasn't a shortage of interesting companies. It was the absence of a consistent, repeatable evaluation lens. Different analysts were applying different mental models, making cross-portfolio comparison difficult and introducing subjectivity into investment conversations.

"The VC instinct is often to pattern-match on the founder or the technology. But early-stage AI startups have a unique failure mode: technically impressive products that collapse on data access, regulatory friction, or GTM ambiguity — none of which shows up in a demo."

The Problem

Early-stage AI startups are notoriously difficult to evaluate because the conventional VC framework — team, market, traction — is insufficient. A model that performs brilliantly in a sandbox can fail spectacularly when deployed at scale due to data quality issues, regulatory exposure, or misaligned distribution channels.

Three structural gaps in the existing evaluation process:

01
No data readiness assessment
Most AI products are only as good as the data they're trained or fine-tuned on. Yet data infrastructure was rarely evaluated systematically during due diligence.
02
Technical architecture treated as a black box
Founders could demo impressive outputs without the team understanding the underlying brittleness — scalability ceilings, latency tradeoffs, or dependency risks.
03
AI governance and safety treated as afterthoughts
For enterprise-facing AI products, the absence of a governance posture is increasingly a deal-breaker for institutional buyers — but wasn't being surfaced in evaluations.

Approach

I conducted a meta-analysis of the existing due diligence process across recent EFL portfolio companies, identifying where post-investment surprises clustered — then worked backward to the evaluation gaps that allowed those surprises through.

Simultaneously, I reviewed published frameworks from leading AI investors (a16z, Sequoia's AI memos, YC evaluation rubrics) and synthesized them against the specific failure modes I'd identified. The goal was a framework that was:

  • Comprehensive enough to surface non-obvious risks
  • Lightweight enough to apply within a standard diligence timeline
  • Comparable across startups at different stages and verticals
  • Defensible in investment committee discussions — not just a checklist

The Framework: 5-Dimension AI Evaluation Model

Each dimension is scored on a 1–5 scale and weighted based on the startup's stage and vertical. The composite score informs, but does not replace, investment judgment.

Product-Market Fit
Is there clear evidence of a specific, high-value problem being solved for a definable customer segment? Is AI the right solution or a hammer looking for nails?
Weight 25%
Technical Viability
Can the architecture scale? What are the latency, cost, and reliability characteristics at 10× current load? Are there hidden single points of failure?
Weight 25%
Data Infrastructure
Does the startup own, license, or have durable access to proprietary data? Is data quality, recency, and labeling sufficient for the claimed performance?
Weight 20%
Governance & Safety
Is there an articulated AI safety posture? For regulated verticals (health, finance, legal), what is the compliance readiness? Is there a responsible AI policy?
Weight 15%
GTM Readiness
Is there a clear go-to-market path? Does the team have distribution relationships, a PLG motion, or enterprise sales experience proportional to the sales cycle?
Weight 15%

Application & Decision Logic

The framework produces not just a composite score but a decision signal. The pattern of scores matters as much as the average — a startup with perfect PMF and GTM scores but a weak data infrastructure score needs a fundamentally different conversation than one with uniform mediocrity.

Score Pattern → Investment Signal
Strong PMF + Strong Tech + Weak Governance

Invest with a governance covenant or advisor requirement. Common in early-stage enterprise AI.

Strong PMF + Weak Tech + Strong Data

Invest if team has credible technical hiring plan. Data moat is the harder asset to build.

Strong Tech + Weak PMF

Pass or defer. Impressive demos without a customer problem is the most common early-stage AI failure mode.

Weak Data + Regulated Vertical

Pass. No data moat + compliance exposure = existential risk the product can't tech its way out of.

Beyond the scoring, I developed GTM roadmap templates for each startup evaluated — giving founders a structured path from current state to first 100 customers, segmented by channel, ICP, and sales motion.

Impact

The evaluation framework was adopted by EFL as a standard component of their due diligence process across subsequent cohort evaluations. Several founders independently applied the framework to structure their own investor pitch narratives — a signal that it resonated beyond the VC context.

Reflection

01
Add Team Execution Score
The framework intentionally excludes "team quality" to avoid confirmation bias, but structured evaluation of domain credibility and execution velocity would improve predictive validity.
02
Governance Audit Framework
The governance dimension was the hardest to score because it's largely self-reported. I'd build a standardized governance questionnaire to create a more auditable signal.
03
Industry-Specific Weighting
I'd develop a sector-specific weighting table — data infrastructure weight should be higher for health AI than for developer tooling, for example. A static weight table misses this nuance.

The key insight that emerged from this engagement: in venture, frameworks don't replace judgment — they make judgment faster and more defensible. The goal isn't to score your way to a decision. It's to surface the right questions before the investment committee conversation, not during it.
